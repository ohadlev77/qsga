Introduction

In this whitepaper I address the applicability of our proposed QSGA method.

We have already formalized a compact Hamiltonian representation of non-trivial exponentially-large graphs, specifically ROP Laplacians. What can we do it?

This issue can be addressed quite broadly, but I am going to focus here on Hamiltonian simulation, for several reasons:

  • Performing Hamiltonian simulation on a quantum computer is exponentially more efficient than the classical alternative, for some classes of (finite dimensional and time-independent) Hamiltonians.
    • It was first shown by Lloyd [Llo96] for $k$-local Hamiltonians.
    • Later on, a generalization for $d$-sparse Hamiltonians was given [AT03], under several assumptions.
    • In the $3$ decades of research since then, many additional Hamiltonian simulation techniques and algorithms were derived [Dal+25].
  • The Hamiltonian simulation routine is considered as the natural computer for a quantum computer to do. The inception of the quantum computation field is rooted in Feynman's belief, among others, that a "quantum mechanical machine" will be able to simulate nature (= Hamiltonians) efficiently [Fey82]. Hamiltonians can be or constructed either simple & easy or complex & hard. Correspondingly, Hamiltonian simulation of "easy-enough" (but still classically-intractable) Hamiltonians is sometimes considered as the first real application that would work on early fault-tolerant quantum computers, or even without error correction, assuming future systems' advances [Chi+18].
  • Hamiltonian simulation is used as the main quantum subroutine in quantum Krylov diagonalization algorithms [ELY22, Yos+25]. It can be of great use to our purpose, since we are interested in computing eigenvalues of graph matrices, e.g. $\lambda_2$ of the graph Laplacian. Quantum Krylov diagonalization algorithms might allow to compute low/high order eigenvalues of exponentially large Hamiltonian matrices.

Hamiltonian Simulation - Definition

In simple words, for finite-dimensional and time-independent Hamiltonians, the quantum Hamiltonian simulation problem can be phrased as:


Given a Hamiltonian $H$, approximately implement the unitary operator $U = e^{-iHt}$ efficiently

The original Hamiltonian simulation techinque introduced in [Llo96] is based on "first-order Trotterization", also called the "Lie-Trotter technique". It is well suited for the case when the Hamiltonians is or can be expressed as a sum of $k$-local operators. It works as follows.

Lie-Trotter (= First Order) Approximated Evolution

It is well known that a unitary evolution for a time span $t$, can be generated from any Hermitian operator Hamiltonian $H$:

$$ U = e^{-iHt} \tag{1} $$

Also, let:

$$ H = \sum_{j = 1}^{L} H_j \tag{2} $$

be a decomposition/construction of the Hamiltonians into $L$ individual $H_j$ Hamiltonian terms. Then:

$$ U = e^{-iHt} = e^{-it \sum_{j=1}^L H_j} \tag{3} $$

It is tempting to transform the sum in the exponent into a prduct of exponents, but it is possible only if all the $H_j$ terms mutually commute. What we can do, in the general case, is to use this transform as an approximation. Lloyd [Llo96] showed that that Trotterization is helpful to approximately simulate & suppress the errors arising from the noncommutativity:

$$ U = \bigg( \prod_{j=1}^{L} e^{-iH_j t / r} \bigg)^r + \underbrace{\sum_{i > j} \big[H_i, H_j \big] \frac{t^2}{2r}}_{2\text{nd Order Commutation Error}} + \underbrace{\sum_{k = 3}^{\infty} E(k)}_{\text{Higher Order Errors}} \tag{4} $$

Where:

  • $t$ is the overall desired simulation time.
  • $r$ is the number of steps we decide to divide the simulation into ("Trotterization").
  • The second order commutation error dominates the overall approximation error. It is suppressed by $r$ - for a fixed $t$, make $r$ large enough, and the error will decay.
    • There is tradeoff here with circuit depth. $r$ grows $\implies$ circuit depth grows $\implies$ decoherence and other noise sources kick in. It can be overcomed with error correction.
  • The higher order errors are shown to be negligible, we will not dive into that in the scope of this whitepaper.

Example for:

$$ \begin{cases} n = 5 \\ H = 0.6XXXIX + 0.8XXIII \\ r = 2 \end{cases} \tag{5} $$

Applicability for QSGA

When I first thought about QSGA, I was inspired from the approaches abundant in simulaiton of Hamiltonians form condensed matter physics, which seems to be based on product-formulas, a.k.a some variants of the original Trotterization introduced by Lloyd.

I am wondering if the other approaches are applicable and if we should dive into this ocean.

What we do need at least for the first paper, is to set bounds/orders for the following:

  • The locality of the skeleton Laplacian $l_s$.
  • The locality of the perturbation $l_p$.
  • The number of perturbations $n_p$.
  • The regularity of the skeleton graph $d$.

From a purely product-formulas approach, it seems that we should define:

$$ l_s \sim O(1) \\ l_p \sim O(1) \\ n_p \sim O(\mathrm{poly}(n)) \\ d \sim O(\mathrm{poly}(n)) \\ $$

We need to describe the influence of noncommutativity of Hamiltonian terms.

Can we employ the $d$-sparse Hamiltonian simulaiton approach to derive stronger scalings/bounds?

DRAFTS

Let a Hamiltonian $H$ have a decomposition into $L$ terms:

$$ H = \sum_{j = 1}^{L} H_j \tag{1} $$

Then the corresponding first-order product formula is:

$$ U_1(t) = \bigg( \prod_{j = 1}^{L} e^{i H_j \frac{t}{r}} \bigg)^r \tag{2} $$

Where $r$ is the number of "Trotter" steps, and $t$ is a time-step of choose.

Why?

  1. We know that given a finite-dimensional & time-independent Hamiltonian $H$, then its unitary evolution is defined by $U = e^{iHt}$, assuming $\hbar = 1$ convention.

  2. The Hamiltonian $H$ has a complete matrix representation by definition (finite-dimensional), but we are more interested in its decomposition/construction from simple terms (Equation (1)), since it is much more practical to derive unitaries that way. So: $$ e^{iHt} = e^{i \sum_j H_j t} = \prod_j e^{i H_j t} + O(t^2) \tag{3} $$ Unless all $H_j$ terms commute (the error free case), then an error on the order of $t^2$ is introduced.

  3. The division to $r$ "Trotter" steps now makes sense, since the "Trotter error" term $O(t^2)$ vanishes as $t$ gets smaller and approaches $0$. So instead of letting the simulation evolve for the desired simulation time, the solution to surpress such Trotter errors is to divide the simulation into $r$ small simulation steps, each of time $t / r$, as seen in Equation (2).

In the simple and rare case where all the $H_j$ terms commute, by simple exponents algebra we can write:

$$ U_{\text{C}} = \prod_{j = 1}^{L} e^{-iH_jt} \tag{4} $$

However, in the general case the terms $H_j$ do not commute, hence an error $\varepsilon$ is introduced into the synthesis of the non-commutative case:

$$ U_{\text{NC}} = U_C + \varepsilon \tag{5} $$

In this notebook we will not dive deep into theory, however what Lloyd showed in [Llo96], based on previous discoveries of others, is that this error can be supressed by dividing the evolution into $r$ small time steps:

$$ U \approx \bigg( \prod_{j=1}^{L} e^{-iH_j \Delta t} \bigg)^r, \quad \quad \mathrm{s.t:} \quad \Delta t = \frac{t}{r} \tag{6} $$
