Interesting Types of Hamiltonians

$k$-local Hamiltonians

$k$-local Hamiltonians are Hamiltonian operators that their decomposed terms act nontrivially on at-most $k$ particles, or qubits in our case. In 1996 it was shown by Lloyd [TODO CITATION] that $k$-local Hamiltonians can be simulated efficiently on a quantum computer.

It is also worth mentioning that as small as $k$ gets, the Hamiltonian simulation circuit would be the leanest. So for near-term, pre fault-tolerance implementation, we might be interseted in very local Hamiltonians, i.e small $k$.

$l$-sparse Hamiltonians

$l$-sparse Hamiltonians are Hamiltonian operatros that their matrix representation has at most $l$ non-zero entries in each row and column. Unlike $k$-local Hamiltonians, in general, $l$-sparse Hamiltonians can't be simulated efficiently. It is possible to simulate $l$-spare Hamiltonians efficiently, as long as one can obtain every entry in the Hamiltonian efficiently [TODO CITATION].

This is interesting. I think it might imply that if we have found a classical way to represent exponentially large non-trivial graphs, then it is also possible to implement an efficient quantum circuit for it.
